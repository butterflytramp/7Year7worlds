The Bug That Built Bangalore

HAT! · 7 Years 7 Worlds · World 02

DEC 31, 1999

The last night of the millennium. The future is translucent, chrome, candy-coloured — and, if the experts are right, about two minutes from collapse.

The Bug

A TIME BOMB
TWO DIGITS WIDE

For decades, memory was precious — so programmers stored years as two digits. “99.” At midnight the counters roll to “00”, and the machines running banks, power grids and planes may decide it is January 1st, 1900.

The Panic

THE WEST DIGS IN

Bunkers stocked. Cash withdrawn. Generators back-ordered and canned food by the pallet. Governments stand up war rooms; the world commits an estimated $300–600 billion to defuse its own shortcut.

Hold your breath

January 1, 2000 · morning

NOTHING BROKE.

The sun came up. The lights stayed on, the planes stayed up — and by nine, kids were out in the park as if the world had never been scheduled to end.

The story usually ends here. Ours turns east.

10½ Hours Ahead

THE CAMERA
NEVER LOOKED EAST

Midnight had already come to Bangalore — and the lights were still on. Not from luck. From years of work the countdown cameras never filmed.

The Night Shift

THE WORLD WAS SAVED
IN THE DARK

COBOL was a dead language in the West — and a living one in India. While America slept, engineers in Bangalore, Chennai, Pune and Hyderabad read the world's legacy code line by line, night after night, for years. No parade was ever planned for them.

The remediation contracts forged the offshore development model — a working proof, at planetary scale, that the world's code could be trusted to India. The industry it ignited now earns the country more than $250 billion a year.

The Prevention Paradox

NOTHING HAPPENED.

Which is why, today, Y2K is remembered as a hoax — as hype, a punchline. The catastrophe was cancelled, so the world decided it had never been real.

How do you get credit
for a disaster you stopped?

Sit with it. The better the job, the more it looks like there was no job at all.

World 02 — Y2K

A MONUMENT TO
INVISIBLE LABOUR

To the night shifts, the retired COBOL hands, the auditors of a hundred million forgotten lines — the people who prevented a boring apocalypse and were repaid with silence. The West forgot what it owes. We didn't.
